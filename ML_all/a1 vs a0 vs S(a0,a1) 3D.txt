*Yes, you are absolutely correct on both points.* Your mathematical surface is completely independent of how you construct the data arrays or which plotting function you use, as long as the $(a_0, a_1, S)$ coordinates remain paired correctly.

Here is exactly what happens in both of those scenarios:

## 1. Swapping the `i` and `j` Loops

If you swap the outer loop (`i`) and the inner loop (`j`), you are essentially just changing the order in which you build the grid.

* *What changes:* Your 2D arrays (`x`, `y`, and `z`) will be *transposed* (rows become columns, and columns become rows).
* *Why it doesn't matter:* Matplotlib's `plot_surface` maps the coordinates based on their indices $(row, column)$. Whether it reads the grid row-by-row or column-by-column, the points in 3D space remain exactly the same. The rendered paraboloid will look identical.

## 2. Using 1D Arrays with `plot_trisurf`

If you decide to ditch the nested lists, append everything to a flat 1D array, and use `f1.plot_trisurf(x, y, z)` instead of `plot_surface`:

* *What changes:* You lose the "structured grid" format. Instead of drawing a neat mesh of quadrilaterals (squares/rectangles) between your points, `plot_trisurf` uses an algorithm (Delaunay triangulation) to connect your flat list of points using a mesh of *triangles*.
* *Why it doesn't matter:* The actual bowl shape of the SSE surface remains completely unchanged. The only difference is aesthetic; the wireframe lines drawn on the surface will be triangular instead of a rectangular grid.

In short, your logic is spot on. The underlying math (the paraboloid) dictates the shape, while loops and Matplotlib functions merely dictate how the pixels are drawn on the screen!
